Introduction

Why are we doing this?

Our Motivation

With heart disease being the leading cause of death in the United States and the costs of healthcare increasing, we wanted to provide people with an easy-to-use and reliable resource to check whether they are at risk of having heart disease.

Exploration

A critical part in building this machine learning algorithm was obtaining a dataset that contained viable and useful information that we could input as part of a teaching set for artificial intelligence. Having accurate predictions is essential for the algorithm to have any possible use, so a dataset was found that contained medical records from patients with and without heart disease, without missing data.However, like most datasets, this one contained some outliers from the rest of the information contained, which when implemented into our algorithm, could lead to inaccurate predictions.

Our steps

So, to help avoid the possibility of inaccurate predictions, we removed data that fell below the first quartile of that particular variable, and any data that exceeded the third quartile to remove any outliers. Standard scaling was also introduced to control the distribution of data so that it had a standard deviation of one. From this data conclusions could be drawn about what typical states of the body would most likely lead to the possibility of contracting this disease.

Extreme Gradient Boosting
Our best model, Extreme Gradient Boosting, or EGB, is a supervised machine learning program that combines many other models, often times weaker models, into its own highly accurate model. EGB is similar to random forest and decision tree models, in the way that each of the models making up EGB makes 1 big tree model with thousands of trees. EGB converts all categorical data into one\-hot encoding, which basically means it prepares the data for the algorithm by assigning the categories 1s and 0s.

K Nearest Neighbor
Our second best model, K-Nearest Neighbor, is a supervised machine learning algorithm that can be used for classification and regression problems. When a data point is given to the algorithm, it then calculates the distance between the given point to the other data points, mostly using Euclidian distance. The nearest “neighbors'' or data points to the given data point are then sorted by distance in increasing order. The data point is then classified among its “k” nearest neighbors. A small “k” value is more accurate but causes overfitting.

Random Forest Classifier
Our third best model, the Random Forest Classifier, is an algorithm that is a meta estimator that takes a certain number of decision tree classifiers onto various sub\-samples of a data set, and uses the mean of the results to determine and improve predictive accuracy.

Results and Conclusion

Ultimately, we have narrowed down the most effective model to be the Extreme Gradient Boost. We based the effectiveness of our models on recall, which scores a model based on the amount of false negatives and true positives. (Formula: TP / (TP + FN) ) We used recall because it’s vital that the model we use minimizes the amount of false negatives, which could leave potential users of our website with a false sense of security. Extreme Gradient Boost was most accurate in terms of recall with a score of 0.972. Other similar “runner up models” scored 0.944 (K-Nearest Neighbor) and 0.963 (Random Forest Classifier) respectively. The majority of models afterwards had scores hovering around 0.95 and lower.

Significance and Extensions

The machine system that we have developed will be critical in allowing a patient to be informed of their health risks and if they should seek medical assistance or not. Since our system is free to use, patients will not need to worry about the medical costs to identify their risk of heart disease, and they can take a preemptive on keeping themselves healthy - getting help first before their situation turns fatal. That is our main goal, to inform people about their health and give some information and awareness on heart disease and the factors that go into it.

While our project works to give accurate predictions most of the time, we can definitely improve our predictions by feeding more datasets in for machine learning. That way, with even more data to cross-examine, it can be as accurate as possible with minimal to no false positives or false negatives. It’s a lot more important to eliminate false negatives overall, though, because telling someone they don’t have heart disease may lead to their disease getting worse.
